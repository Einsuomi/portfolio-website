# EU Blue Card — Mobility Rules

**Type:** learning
**Access:** public
**Tags:** EU-Blue-Card, immigration, Luxembourg, mobility, work-permit
**Updated:** 2026-04-27
**tldr:** Luxembourg Blue Card requires €65,652+ gross salary; 12-month mobility rule from Finland already met; Switzerland incompatible with EU Blue Card entirely.
**Related:** [[relocation-decision-luxembourg]]

## Summary
The EU Blue Card is a work and residence permit for highly skilled non-EU nationals working in an EU member state. After 12 months of legal residence with a valid EU Blue Card, holders can move to Luxembourg under a simplified procedure. The Luxembourg salary floor is €65,652/year gross — a legal requirement, not just a negotiation target. Switzerland is incompatible with the EU Blue Card system entirely.

## Detail

### What the EU Blue Card Is
Issued by EU member states to non-EU nationals with a qualifying job offer meeting a salary threshold. Combines work permit and residence permit. Holder can bring family (spouse + dependent children) under derived rights.

### Luxembourg-Specific Salary Threshold
**€65,652/year gross minimum** as of March 2026 — set by Grand-Ducal regulation, updated annually. History: €63,408 (March 2025) → €65,652 (March 2026). Trend is upward each year. Any job offer below this figure disqualifies the applicant from the Luxembourg EU Blue Card — it is a legal constraint, not just a career target. Target meaningfully above the floor (€70k+) to protect against future threshold increases at renewal.

### The 12-Month Mobility Rule
**Prerequisite:** have held a valid EU Blue Card issued by any EU member state for at least 12 months, and have legally resided in that country during those 12 months.

Two application paths for Luxembourg:
1. **Apply from Finland** (before entering Luxembourg) — submit the application while still resident in Finland, then enter Luxembourg once approved
2. **Apply after arrival** — enter Luxembourg first, then submit the application within **1 month** of arrival

Either path is valid. The employer may mandate a third party (e.g. an immigration lawyer or HR) to handle the procedures.

**Cost:** €80 (bank transfer to Luxembourg Ministry of Home Affairs)
**Contact:** immigration.public@mai.etat.lu / (+352) 247 84 040

### Does Changing Employers Reset the 12-Month Clock?
**No.** The 12-month requirement is tied to legal residence while holding a valid EU Blue Card — not to employment with a specific employer. Under the 2021 EU Blue Card Directive, the mobility right is personal. Changing employers in Finland does not restart the clock. The clock runs from when the Blue Card was first issued and legal residence began. This means: leaving Basware before finding a Luxembourg job does not affect mobility eligibility, as long as the card remains valid.

Note: changing employers within the first 12 months in the issuing country requires notifying authorities; after 12 months the holder can change employer freely.

### Application Requirements Summary
- Valid passport
- EU Blue Card held for 12+ months (from another EU member state)
- Employment contract of **at least 6 months** duration
- Annual gross salary of **at least €65,652**
- Proof of professional qualifications matching the role
- Criminal record documentation
- Proof of suitable housing in Luxembourg
- Submit within 1 month of entering Luxembourg (or before entry)

### Employer Obligations — Why It's Easy for Them Too
Luxembourg EU Blue Card hires are **exempt from the labor market test** — the employer does NOT need to prove no suitable EU/EEA candidate was available first. This is the main friction in standard non-EU hiring and it simply doesn't apply here.

Full employer obligations:
1. Declare vacant position to ADEM (simple notification, not an approval)
2. Sign a contract ≥6 months at ≥€65,652
3. Request copy of the worker's authorization to stay
4. Notify immigration within 3 working days of the first day of work (worker name, social security number, start date)

For large Luxembourg employers (banks, Big 4, consulting, EU-adjacent tech): entirely routine — they have HR teams that handle this regularly. For smaller employers hiring non-EU for the first time: still straightforward, may use an immigration lawyer (€500–1,500, typically employer-paid). Getting the job offer is 100x harder than processing the permit.

### Why Switzerland Is Ruled Out
Switzerland is not an EU member state. The EU Blue Card has no validity there. As a non-EU national moving to Switzerland requires the Swiss cantonal B permit system — quota-based, employer-dependent, unpredictable. Even EU citizens use a different (bilateral treaty) pathway. For a non-EU Blue Card holder, Switzerland is dramatically harder than any EU destination.
